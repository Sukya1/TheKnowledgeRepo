9 Things I wish I knew before becoming a Computer Science Major

by: Sukya Williams

1. Grades are not a reflection of your worth

I know! I know! Most of us have heard countless amount of times that grades are important but they are alot less important than you think. Take it from someone who has spent many hours worrying about grades, it's not worth it. This time could be better spent improving your coding skills, networking, getting to know your professors and teaching assitant - all things that are just as important as your grades.

2. There is an immense amount of resources available to students

Everyday I come across new communities and programs that support computer science students of various backgrounds. The key to finding these resources is keeping up to date with linkedin and joining communities that will support your career development. Here are a few commmunities to keep in mind:

Organisations Initiative
Codepath Courses, Internships, Mentorship
Colorstack Networking, Professional Development
Code2040 Internships, Networking, Mentorship
MLT Career Development, Networking, Fellowship
Major League Hacking Hackathon Opportunties

3. Linkedin is your best friend

It took me 2 years in college, desperstion and a major change to realize the importance of Linkedin for people I tech. Linkedin is great for staying up to date on opportunities availabele to students. It's also a great way to learn about organisations that support student growth and guide students to a career in tech. One strategy that I have used to get an idea of what programs are available was to look at the profiles of other students who had a similar interest as mine and see if I was interested in any of the program. The great thing about linkedin is that it makes reccomendations about people and post that align with what you have viewed. With these reccomendations I have learned about opportunities such as the Google Bold Internship and Immersion Program and organisations such as ColorStack and Codepath. If you haven't done so already make your linkedin account today, update it and use it be more aware of what is going on in the tech industry.

4. Join a club that will improve your skills

Join a tech-related club! For a long time, I put this off because I felt like I was always busy but the truth is not being in a club like this really prevented me from reaching the career milestones that I should have. Many schools have clubs like Hack4Impact that work on websites and apps for non-profits. These clubs are a great way to learn new-skills that could be useful for creating your own projects and improving your resume. It is also a great way to meet other CS students who could potentially be in your classes.

5. Apply for internships early and know where to find them

Tech internships tend to have an earlier timeline compared to other forms internships. The recruitment process ususally begins the summer and fall period before the internship. These applications are usually on a rolling basis so appying earlier usually gives you a better chance of getting the position. Oftentimes people compile a list of internships available on github. Ususally, searching for the time period followed by tech internship produces a repository with this information. It could also be useful to get referrals from older computer science students, this could make it easier for your application to be seen by hiring managers.

6. Get to know your professors

Go to Office Hours! Office Hours is a great way to get to know your professor. Most professors are really interested in their students and want to know more about you. I lnow it's a bit nerve racking but i've come to learn that most professors are great at keeping a conversation going. There are benefits to getting to know professor, they will be more willing to write you a letter of reccomendation if they know you personally and have great connection to possibly help you find interships and even jobs. They are also a great resource for career advice.

7. Freshman/ Pre-freshman year is not too early

If you are one of those students who is looking for opportunities as a pre-freshman or freshman, there are opportunities fo you too. Companies like Google, MLT and Netflix offer opprtunites that help students pave the way for successful careers. These are great for building your network and adding valuable experiences to your resume.

8. Github is a Must

GitHub is a tool that is used by every software developer. It is great for working on projects as teams and a skill that every intern should have. It is also where most people host the files for their projects and showcase them to potential employers. It is very doable to set up a profile and begin using it on your own. This freecodecamp video helped me get started and allowed me to start my first project using vs code on my own.

9. Practise, Practise, Practise

The technical interview requires you to have a strong understanding of data structures and algorithns. A great way to prepare for this is to practise coding problems. Lucky for you, there are great resources availabe to help you prepare. These include but are not limited to leetcode, algoexpert and hackerrank. There is also a list of questions known as the blind75 that are said to be the most helpful in allowing you to identify common patterns i the technical interview. One noteable book to keep in mind is Cracking the Coding Interview. It not only gives advice on the cracking the behavioral and technical interview, but it also gives a review of each topic and practise question as well as pointers on how to negotiate an offer.
